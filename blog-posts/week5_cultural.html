Pairing and Giving Feedback - 09.06.2014

One emotion that arose during my pairing session
While I am working with a partner on a pair session, I run through a gambit of emotions. The emotion that stands out above all others is excitement. I'm excited to work with someone new on a project. I like to learn more about them and find a way that we can work best. I also like to find out what's their thought process about a particular problem and compare what I had in mind.

What is kind, actionable, and specific feedback?
What is a kind feedback?
Kind feedback is honest feedback.

What is actionable feedback?
An actionable feedback would suggest a future course of action.

What is specific feedback?
Give specific examples of the behavior in question. It may have made a strong impression on you, but the person receiving feedback may not have noticed anything unusual. However, don't let examples distract from your core point--people sometimes respond to examples by giving excuses for that specific instance. You can say, "This is one example that shows the behavior I mentioned, but I've noticed it other times."

My feeling of vulnerablity when pairing and reading feedback
While working in a pair session, there is a sense of vulnerability that creeps into you psyche. Sometimes I wonder if I know enough to effectively contribute to the project. In the back of my mind, the DBC has done a great job at putting everyone on the same page and establishing that we should check our ego at the door. In the back of my mind, I know we are all here to learn and there are students that will come in with more knowledge; others less. I know that I will not know everything and when I don't know, I am comfortable saying so. But I'm not afraid to do the research to find out the answer. I also know what type of learning and thinking style I'm most effective at and I let my partner know. So at the end of my pairing sessions, I'm happy to hear where I need to improve from a third person's perspective. With that knowledge, I can work towards improving the "holes in my game".

When reading feedback, there is the same kind of vulnerability I've expressed above. Sometimes, getting feedback from a live person can be a bit "sugar-coated" because no one wants to hurt another person's feelings. In written feedback, people don't hold back. Regardless, I approach all of the feedback the same. In football terms, I can use tape (code) to evaluate my performance, but having a scouting report AND the tape is even better. Still, the waiting game for feedback is a bit unnerving. I mean, who likes to wait right?!
